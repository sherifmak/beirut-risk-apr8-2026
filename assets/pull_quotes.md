## The headline claim (use as the hero pull quote)

> **For the average resident of Beirut city proper, Tuesday carried roughly
> the same daily risk of death that the average New Yorker carried on
> September 11, 2001.**

> **Tuesday was a 9/11 for a city the size of Beirut.**

> **302 and 372 are the same number.** They sit on top of each other on a
> log-scale chart of days the world remembers.

## The port explosion equivalence (for a Lebanese audience)

> For the average Greater Beirut resident, April 8, 2026 was **within a
> rounding error of August 4, 2020**. Same city, same denominator.

> We all know exactly where we were at 6:07 PM on August 4, 2020.
> Tuesday was a day of that shape.

---

## The warning against rounding down

> If you want to know how dangerous Tuesday was *for a city*, the number
> is roughly 9/11 — and I would not trust anyone whose instinct is to
> round that down.

> Citywide averages are useful for scale. They are useless for personal
> decision-making.

## The methodology-as-emotional-self-defense line

> The honest thing to do with a hard feeling is to try to put bounds
> around it — not to make it smaller, but to make it *the right size*.

> I computed it because the alternative was to sit with an
> undifferentiated *bad* I couldn't reason about.

## Caveats to quote (so they get shared alongside the headline)

> This is a citywide average. It is the wrong number for any individual
> person's safety — including anyone you love.

> Residents of a block that took a direct hit faced a risk probably a
> hundred to a thousand times higher than the average.

> The Lebanese Ministry of Public Health says the 300+ figure is
> preliminary and expected to rise. Any number here is a lower bound.
