# Beirut just had a 9/11-scale day

*I did the math because the news couldn't tell me what I needed to know.*

---

On Tuesday, April 8 2026, Israel launched its largest wave of airstrikes on
Lebanon of the current war. Fifty fighter jets. A hundred and sixty
munitions. A hundred targets in ten minutes. Central Beirut, without
warning, across at least five neighborhoods. Southern suburbs. The Bekaa
Valley. A funeral procession.

More than three hundred people killed. More than a thousand six hundred
wounded. Totals still rising.

I have family and friends in Beirut. I wanted to know what Tuesday actually
*meant* for them — not as a headline, but as a number I could compare to
something I already understood. Something in my bones.

I computed the answer three different ways to make sure I hadn't fooled
myself. All three agreed on the scale.

**For the average resident of Beirut city proper, Tuesday carried roughly
the same daily risk of death that the average New Yorker carried on September
11, 2001.**

Not metaphorically. Not rhetorically. Statistically. Same math. Same unit.
Same order of magnitude. Tuesday was a 9/11 for a city the size of Beirut.

I want to explain what that claim means, why it's a defensible one, and
what it does and does not say.

## The unit

There's a unit for this kind of comparison. It's called a **micromort** —
one chance in a million of death. A Stanford decision analyst invented it
in the 1970s because human brains are bad at tiny probabilities: we round
rare to never and common to always, and we refuse to do arithmetic on
fractions smaller than 1 in 100. Micromorts fix this by turning small
probabilities into whole numbers. Some anchors:

- Driving 100 km on a highway: **~0.4 µM**
- One skydive: **~8 µM**
- A normal day of being alive, healthy 30-year-old: **~25 µM**
- A normal day of being alive, healthy 60-year-old: **~300 µM**

You can compute a micromort for any day, any activity, any event — traffic,
war, disease — and put them side by side. That's the whole point.

## How 9/11 and Tuesday compare

Start with 9/11. About 2,977 people were killed on September 11, 2001,
almost all in New York City. The city proper had ~8 million residents.
That's a daily risk of death of:

```
2,977 / 8,000,000 × 1,000,000 = ~372 µM
```

**Roughly 372 micromorts for the average New Yorker on 9/11.** About
fifteen times a normal day. A single-day spike the country has spent two
decades trying to metabolize.

Now Tuesday in Beirut. The Lebanese Ministry of Public Health's updated
total is 300+ killed nationwide, with the Civil Defense's district
breakdown showing 92 killed in the Beirut governorate (the municipality
proper) and 61 in the southern suburbs of Dahiyeh. Scale the Civil Defense
district shares by the Health Ministry uplift and you get ~109 killed in
Beirut city proper. The population of the Beirut governorate is ~361,000.
That's:

```
109 / 361,000 × 1,000,000 = ~302 µM
```

**Roughly 302 micromorts for the average Beirut-proper resident on Tuesday.**

372 and 302 are the same number. They are within a rounding error of each
other, and both are dwarfed by the two-orders-of-magnitude range that
separates 9/11 from a normal day. If you place them on a log scale of
daily-risk events, they sit on top of each other.

That is the central claim of this post. Tuesday, in Beirut, was a 9/11.

## The full picture — in one table

The 9/11 comparison isn't cherry-picked. It's the middle of a range. Here's
what April 8 looks like next to other days the world remembers, all
computed the same way — deaths ÷ population most exposed × 1,000,000:

| Event | Pop. denominator | Risk (µM) |
|---|---:|---:|
| London 7/7 bombings (2005) | London, 7.5M | ~7 |
| Paris Nov 13 attacks (2015) | Paris metro, 12M | ~11 |
| Peak COVID day, NYC (Apr 7 2020) | NYC, 8M | ~100 |
| **Beirut port explosion (Aug 4 2020)** | **Greater Beirut, 2.2M** | **~99** |
| **Beirut Apr 8 2026 — Greater Beirut metro** | **2.2M** | **~82** |
| 9/11 attacks — NYC metro basis | 19M | ~157 |
| **Beirut Apr 8 2026 — city proper** | **361,000** | **~302** |
| **9/11 attacks — NYC city basis** | **8M** | **~372** |
| Hiroshima atomic bomb (1945) | 340,000 | ~200,000 |

Three things fall out of this table.

**First**, April 8 in Beirut was in **the same league as 9/11**. At the
city-proper level the numbers are nearly identical (302 vs 372 µM). At the
metro level Beirut is roughly half of NYC (82 vs 157 µM). Either way, the
daily-risk order of magnitude is the same.

**Second**, April 8 was, for the average Greater Beirut resident, **within a
rounding error of August 4, 2020**. Same city, same denominator, ~82 vs ~99
µM. Beirutis do not need anyone to explain what the port explosion was. We
know exactly where we were at 6:07 PM that day. Tuesday was a day of that
shape.

**Third**, even the governorate number (302 µM) is **fifteen hundred times
smaller than Hiroshima**. That is worth saying out loud, not because it
minimizes anything, but because the unit only works if you respect its
range. Days of war have an enormous dynamic range. April 8 was a very bad
day. It was not every bad day.

## Why the "average" is load-bearing — and a lie

Now the caveat that has to be read, not skimmed.

The 302-micromort answer is a **mean across 361,000 people**. It is the
number you get when you pretend the risk was spread evenly, when it
absolutely was not. The strikes hit specific buildings. A resident of a
block that took a direct hit faced a risk probably a hundred to a thousand
times higher than the average. A resident of a quiet neighborhood that
wasn't targeted faced something close to a normal day. The average collapses
both realities into a number that belongs to neither person.

Citywide averages are useful for scale. They are useless for personal
decision-making. If you want to know how dangerous Tuesday was *for a city*,
the number is roughly 9/11 — and I would not trust anyone whose instinct is
to round that down. If you want to know how dangerous Tuesday was for your
cousin whose building is two streets from where a bomb landed, the
statistics have nothing to offer, and the number you want does not exist.

## What the number doesn't capture

Hospital-overload deaths that haven't happened yet. Displacement. Water,
electricity, healthcare. What it does to a child to hear a sonic boom at
2:30 PM on a Tuesday and not know if the next minute is the news. Grief
that will propagate through families for a generation. The war before this
day, the war after.

It is a single thin statistical sliver of a single day.

I computed it because the alternative was to sit with an undifferentiated
*bad* I couldn't reason about. The honest thing to do with a hard feeling
is to try to put bounds around it — not to make it smaller, but to make it
*the right size*. The right size of this one turned out to be **9/11 in New
York** and **August 4, 2020 in Beirut**, sitting on top of each other on a
log-scale chart.

I didn't feel better afterward. But I understood what I was afraid of with
a little more precision. That was what I came for.
